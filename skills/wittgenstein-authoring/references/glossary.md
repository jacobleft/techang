# Glossary

**Boundary** — The limit between what a document can responsibly say and what it must omit, mark as unknown, or show indirectly (Ch 1, Ch 4).

**Cardinal proposition** — A root claim that governs a branch of subordinate clarifications; use only in high-rigor mode (Ch 7).

**Clarification** — The activity of making a thought clear, bounded, and usable rather than merely stating doctrine (Ch 4).

**Decimal tree** — Wittgenstein's hierarchical proposition structure; a restricted authoring mode for exceptional logical sharpness (Ch 7).

**Elucidation** — A sentence, example, structure, or scaffold that helps the reader reach clarity (Ch 4, Ch 5).

**Fact / state of affairs** — What is the case; for writing, the situation a claim or model tries to represent (Ch 6).

**General form: “This is how things stand”** — A test that a sentence states a situation rather than merely signalling significance (Ch 4).

**High-rigor mode** — Tractatus-level proposition hierarchy; ask before using it because it is too rigid for most daily and professional writing (Ch 7).

**Internal relation** — A relation shown by structure rather than asserted from outside (Ch 2).

**Ladder** — Scaffolding that lets readers reach a difficult concept; not always disposable, sometimes mandatory (Ch 5).

**Logical picture** — A representation that preserves relevant structure with what it represents (Ch 6).

**Logical syntax** — Rules for term use and combination that prevent conceptual confusion (Ch 3).

**Mode of signification** — The way a sign functions in context; the same word may have different modes (Ch 3).

**Overclaiming** — Saying more than the evidence, terms, medium, or scope can support (Ch 1).

**Picture** — A model, example, diagram, or prose representation that shares relevant form with a target situation (Ch 6).

**Proposition with sense** — A statement clear enough to agree or disagree with a possible situation (Ch 4).

**Scaffolding** — Definitions, examples, sequence, diagrams, warnings, or prerequisites that support reader understanding (Ch 5).

**Showing** — Making meaning visible through form, structure, example, behavior, or relation rather than direct assertion (Ch 2).

**Sign** — The visible word, string, label, or notation (Ch 3).

**Silence** — Disciplined refusal to say what cannot be said clearly in the present document (Ch 1).

**Symbol** — A sign in use with a specific sense and role (Ch 3).

**Term discipline** — Stable, explicit control of load-bearing terms; one concept per role, overloads marked (Ch 3).
