# Chapter 2: Saying vs Showing

## Core Idea

Do not force every meaning into explicit statement. Some authorial work is done by structure, examples, sequence, formatting, constraints, omissions, and behavior.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Saying vs Showing**
  - When to use: when deciding whether to state a point directly or embody it in the document.
  - How: say what readers need as propositions; show what readers need as relations, patterns, priorities, examples, or demonstrations.
- **Internal relation display**
  - When to use: when the relationship between parts matters more than a sentence asserting that relationship.
  - How: place related items together, sequence dependencies before dependents, use consistent headings, or build examples whose structure displays the relation.

## Key Concepts

- **Saying**: explicitly stating a proposition, claim, rule, requirement, or decision.
- **Showing**: making a relation or form visible through arrangement or example.
- **Logical form**: what the expression must share with the thing it represents in order to represent it.
- **Internal property/relation**: a relation that shows itself through the thing's structure rather than through an external assertion.
- **Display**: arranging material so the reader can see how it works.

## Mental Models

- Say the **contract**; show the **competence**.
- Say **definitions and constraints**; show **judgment and priority**.
- Say **what must be acted on**; show **what must be recognized**.
- Treat examples as pictures: they should have enough shared structure with the target case to teach without extra explanation.

## Anti-patterns

- **Saying what structure already shows**: adding meta-commentary like “this is rigorous” instead of writing rigorously.
- **Showing what must be said**: hiding requirements, deadlines, assumptions, or decisions inside tone or examples.
- **Decorative examples**: examples that do not share the relevant structure with the claim.
- **Trust claims**: saying “reliable,” “clear,” or “high quality” where the document should demonstrate reliability, clarity, or quality.

## Key Takeaways

1. Explicitness is not always clarity.
2. Structure can carry meaning more cleanly than explanation.
3. If readers must act on it, say it.
4. If readers must learn a pattern, show it.
5. The document's form should prove its own priorities.
6. In AI-agent skills, show operational priorities through ordering, triggers, and examples; do not rely only on abstract values.

## Connects To

- **Ch 1**: silence decides what cannot be said.
- **Ch 3**: term discipline makes saying possible.
- **Ch 5**: scaffolding often shows before it says.
- **Ch 6**: pictures and models are disciplined forms of showing.
