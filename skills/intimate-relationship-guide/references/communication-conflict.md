# Communication, Stresses, and Conflict

This document covers the dynamics of interaction, friction, and conflict in relationships.

## Communication
- **Nonverbal Communication**: Includes facial expressions, gazing, body language, touch, and interpersonal distance. Sensitivity to a partner's nonverbal cues predicts relationship satisfaction.
- **Verbal Communication**: Self-disclosure (sharing personal information) builds intimacy.
- **Dysfunctional Patterns**:
  - *Kitchen-sinking*: Addressing several topics at once so everything gets distracted.
  - *Off-beam*: Wandering from topic to topic.
  - *Mindreading*: Assuming you understand the partner's thoughts.
  - *Cross-complaining*: Responding to a complaint with your own complaint.
  - *Four Horsemen (Gottman)*: Criticism, Contempt, Defensiveness, Stonewalling.
- **Healthy Communication**:
  - Use **XYZ Statements**: "When you do X in situation Y, I feel Z."
  - **Active Listening**: Paraphrase what the partner said and validate their perspective.

## Stresses and Strains
- **Perceived Relational Value**: Hurt feelings arise when we perceive that others value us less than we want them to.
- **Jealousy**: Reactive jealousy (response to an actual threat) vs. Suspicious jealousy (unfounded worry).
- **Betrayal**: Disagreeable, hurtful actions by people we trusted. Betrayers often underestimate the harm they do.
- **Forgiveness**: Occurs more readily when the offender is sincerely apologetic and the victim is empathetic. It is beneficial for the victim's own well-being.

## Conflict
- Conflict is inevitable because partners have different moods and preferences, and certain tensions are woven into the fabric of relationships (e.g., autonomy vs. connection).
- **Course of Conflict**: Begins with an instigating event (criticism, illegitimate demands, rebuffs, cumulative annoyances).
- **Responses**:
  - *Voice* (active, constructive)
  - *Loyalty* (passive, constructive)
  - *Exit* (active, destructive)
  - *Neglect* (passive, destructive)
- **Volatile vs. Validator vs. Avoider**: Different conflict styles can work as long as the ratio of positive to negative interactions remains at least 5-to-1. Hostile styles are always detrimental.

## Power and Violence
- **Power**: The ability to influence the behavior of others. Men typically have more power due to relative resources and social norms, but relationships are happier when power is shared equally.
- **Violence**: Intimate partner violence comes in forms: Situational Couple Violence (erupts from heated conflicts), Intimate Terrorism (one partner uses violence to control and oppress), and Violent Resistance (fighting back against intimate terrorism).
