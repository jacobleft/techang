# Maintenance, Repair, and Dissolution

This document provides guidance on keeping relationships strong, fixing them when they are broken, or navigating their end.

## Maintaining and Enhancing Relationships
- **Cognitive Maintenance Mechanisms**:
  - *Cognitive Interdependence*: Thinking of yourselves as "us" instead of "me" and "you".
  - *Positive Illusions*: Idealizing the partner.
  - *Perceived Superiority*: Believing your relationship is better than most.
  - *Inattention to Alternatives*: Ignoring potential other partners.
  - *Derogation of Tempting Alternatives*: Judging potential alternatives as less attractive.
- **Behavioral Maintenance Mechanisms**:
  - *Willingness to Sacrifice*: Putting the partner's needs first.
  - *Michelangelo Phenomenon*: Encouraging the partner to be all they can be.
  - *Accommodation*: Tolerating minor provocations without retaliating.
  - *Self-Control*: Managing impulses.
  - *Play*: Doing novel, exciting things together.
  - *Forgiveness*: Letting go of grudges.

## Repairing Relationships
- **Do It Yourself**: Requires deliberate effort to change dysfunctional patterns (e.g., reading books, actively practicing XYZ statements, prioritizing positivity).
- **Therapy**: Several approaches exist (e.g., Traditional Behavioral Couple Therapy, Cognitive-Behavioral Couple Therapy, Emotionally Focused Couple Therapy). Therapy is highly effective, especially when couples seek it *before* problems become severe.

## The Dissolution and Loss of Relationships
- **Predictors of Divorce**: Low socioeconomic status, marrying young, prior marriage, premarital cohabitation, and having divorced parents increase risk. However, the most critical factors are enduring vulnerabilities, stressful events, and poor adaptation to those events.
- **Breaking Up**: Often gradual. People use different strategies (direct vs. indirect, other-oriented vs. self-oriented). The most common pattern is "preserving indirectness"—gradual dissatisfaction without explicit attempts to repair the relationship.
- **The Aftermath**:
  - Breakups are painful but people tend to overestimate how long the pain will last.
  - Adjustment takes time, but most people eventually recover and become as happy as they were before.
  - Post-divorce life is complex, often involving economic hardship for women and ongoing contact through co-parenting. Coparenting works best when parents can separate their past marital conflict from their current parenting responsibilities.
