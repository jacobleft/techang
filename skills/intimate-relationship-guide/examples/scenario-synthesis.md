# Scenario Synthesis Example

This example demonstrates how to synthesize empathy with scientific relationship advice based on the references provided.

**User Input:**
"My partner and I have been arguing every night over stupid things like whose turn it is to do the dishes. It feels like we just keep score. It's exhausting."

**Agent Response:**

"I'm really sorry to hear that you're going through this. Arguing every night is indeed exhausting, and feeling like you're both 'keeping score' can make you feel more like adversaries than partners. It is completely normal for relationships to experience friction, especially around household chores, but the way we handle that friction makes all the difference.

Here are a few insights from relationship science that might help you shift this dynamic:

1. **Recognize the Pattern of 'Keeping Score'**: It sounds like your relationship might be operating on an 'exchange' basis right now, where each partner expects immediate repayment for their efforts. Healthy, long-term relationships usually operate on a 'communal' basis, where partners respond to each other's needs without strictly tracking who owes what.
2. **Beware of Negative Attributions**: When we are frustrated, we often attribute our partner's negative behavior to their character (e.g., 'they are lazy') while excusing our own (e.g., 'I am just tired'). Try to assume positive intent or consider situational factors affecting your partner.
3. **Change the Way You Communicate**: When bringing up the dishes next time, avoid 'kitchen-sinking' (bringing up past complaints) and try using an 'XYZ Statement'. For example: *'When you leave the dishes in the sink after dinner, I feel overwhelmed and unsupported.'*
4. **Boost the Positivity**: John Gottman's research suggests that stable couples maintain a ratio of 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative interaction during conflict. Find small ways to show appreciation and warmth outside of the arguments.

Try to find a calm moment, perhaps on a weekend morning when you aren't tired, to sit down together and address this pattern. Would you like to practice drafting what you want to say to them?"
